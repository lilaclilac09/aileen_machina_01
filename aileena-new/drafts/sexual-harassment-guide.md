# Sexual Harassment, Power, and How to Defend Yourself
### A guide for anyone working in industries where power and money concentrate

*Draft — not published. Written by Aileena.*

## It Almost Always Comes From Someone You Know

This is the first thing to understand — and the reason it's so hard to name when it's happening.

Research consistently shows that the majority of sexual harassment, assault, and workplace coercion is perpetrated by someone the victim already knows: a colleague, a manager, a mentor, a client, someone from the same network. The U.S. Equal Employment Opportunity Commission reports that in workplace settings, harassment is overwhelmingly committed by people in existing power relationships with the target — not strangers.

This matters because the "stranger danger" model is wrong. It doesn't prepare you for what actually happens, which is: someone you've spoken to, someone you may have trusted, someone with social proximity to you, using that access as a weapon.

The familiarity is not accidental. It is the mechanism. You are less likely to react sharply to someone you know. You are less likely to be believed if they are well-regarded. You are more likely to doubt yourself — *was I misreading it? we've worked together before.* That confusion is part of how control works.

## Sex and Power Are Used as Instruments of Deterioration — Deliberately

Harassment is not impulsive. It is not a compliment that went too far. Organizational psychology research, including foundational work on power and social hierarchy (Fiske, 1993; Pratto & Walker, 2004), documents that individuals in positions of power frequently use sexual behavior as a dominance signal — not out of attraction, but out of control-seeking.

The quid pro quo pattern — *"sleep with me and I'll bring you 100M in volume," "I'll introduce you to X if you..."* — is documented extensively in finance, tech, music, and every industry where gatekeeping exists. It is a calculated exchange offer that exploits professional vulnerability. It is designed to make you feel that your career depends on your compliance.

The physical version — a hand on a shoulder, a body too close, an unexpected squeeze — functions the same way. It tests how you respond. It establishes hierarchy without words. It reads your reaction to measure how much resistance you will offer.

I was at an industry event. Standing alone. A man I didn't know walked up behind me and placed his hand on my shoulder — unexpectedly, deliberately — and squeezed. Not a greeting. A signal. Senior people nearby saw it. Nobody said anything. Nobody moved. So I stopped defending myself, and I kept moving.

Both methods — the proposition and the physical test — share the same logic: **use a woman's need for professional safety against her.**

## Why You May Not Defend Yourself — And Why That Is Not Your Failure

There is a physiological and psychological reason why many women freeze, go silent, or disengage in the moment harassment occurs — and why they often don't report it afterward.

The trauma response — freeze, fawn, or flight — is not weakness. It is a nervous system response to perceived threat where the brain calculates that overt resistance may escalate danger. In environments where senior people are present and doing nothing, the brain also calculates: *there is no institutional support here.* Both assessments are often accurate.

Psychologist Judith Herman's research on coercive control documents that environments of repeated low-level intimidation — the kind that never quite crosses a line visible enough for others to act on — progressively erode a person's sense of what they are entitled to defend. This is not an accident of the situation. It is a predictable outcome of the design.

If you have been in this position — if you saw it happening to you, felt it clearly, and still didn't act — you were not weak. You were responding rationally to an environment that had already told you it would not back you.

## Supervisory Ambiguity: When Your Boss Uses Attraction as a Management Tool

This is a particularly difficult form of harassment to name, because it is designed to look like it isn't harassment at all.

A superior creates emotional closeness with a female subordinate that exceeds professional boundaries — private messages unrelated to work, comments that blend professional feedback with personal interest, suggestions of a "special understanding" between you that other colleagues don't have. No single incident is clear enough to report. But the cumulative pattern is: **using emotional ambiguity to create a sense of obligation.**

Organizational psychology calls this supervisory emotional coercion. The mechanism: because the superior controls performance reviews, assignments, and promotions, any emotional ambiguity they introduce carries an implicit professional dimension. You don't need to be told explicitly. The structure says it for them. You begin to carry that variable into every interaction — *is this evaluation about my work, or about something else?* — and that weight is exactly the point.

The ambiguity is the weapon. It is designed to make you doubt your own perception: *maybe I'm imagining it, maybe he's like this with everyone, maybe I'm making it awkward by pulling back.* This self-doubt consumes energy, creates caution, and can produce involuntary accommodation — all outcomes that benefit the person in power.

Sociologist Catharine MacKinnon documented this dynamic as early as 1979: sexual power in workplaces frequently operates through deniable intimacy rather than explicit demand, precisely because ambiguity protects the harasser while isolating the target.

**Warning signs:**
- Communication volume and content that exceeds professional necessity
- Personal compliments embedded in professional feedback
- Language suggesting a special relationship that excludes other colleagues
- Unusually personal interest in your career tied to his involvement
- Noticeable cooling or professional consequences when you maintain distance

**What you can do:** Move all communication to documented channels. Decline solo meetings without explanation — simply propose alternatives with others present. If he responds to your professional distance with reduced opportunity or visible displeasure, that response is itself evidence that the relationship was never purely professional.

## When Defending Yourself Becomes the Offense: Retaliation as Authority Restoration

This is the pattern that most people are not prepared for, and it is among the most damaging.

You set a boundary. You refused. You reported. You defended yourself, professionally and clearly. And then — the person who harassed you, or the institution protecting them, responded by stripping something from you: your role, your responsibilities, your access, your reputation in the room.

This is not an accident or an overreaction on their part. Research on power and dominance (Kipnis, 1976; Fiske & Berdahl, 2007) shows that individuals who use power for control experience boundary-setting or reporting by a subordinate as a direct challenge to their authority. The harassment was an assertion of hierarchy. Your refusal inverted it. Their retaliation is an attempt to restore it — to demonstrate to themselves and the surrounding environment that the hierarchy remains intact.

This is why retaliation is often disproportionate to what you actually did. You didn't attack them. You simply refused. But to someone whose sense of control depends on your compliance, refusal is an attack.

**The forms retaliation takes:**
- Sudden negative performance reviews that did not exist before
- Being excluded from meetings, projects, or communications
- Public undermining — being contradicted in front of colleagues, having contributions attributed to others
- Informal reputation damage — *"she's difficult," "she's not a team player," "she has personal issues"*
- Structural demotion without formal title change: same role, stripped of actual function
- Creating conditions designed to make you leave voluntarily

**What this means legally:**
Retaliation for refusing harassment or for making a complaint is illegal under employment law in most jurisdictions, independently of whether the original harassment claim succeeds. Document the transition point precisely. If your treatment shifted after a specific refusal or complaint — record that incident, record the date, record what changed. This sequence is your case.

Do not resign. Do not sign anything characterizing your departure as voluntary. Consult a labor attorney before making any employment decision once retaliation has begun.

## Distinguishing Workplace Pressure, Racial Discrimination, and Sexual Harassment

These three things can coexist, overlap, and be deliberately confused by the people using them.

**Workplace competition pressure** is legitimate when applied consistently across comparable roles. It becomes a tool of harassment when selectively applied to you — particularly after a refusal or complaint — or designed to be impossible to meet so your "failure" provides cover for removing you.

**Racial discrimination** targets you based on ethnicity, nationality, or race: differential treatment in assignments, pay, promotion, or daily interaction. Held to different standards. Excluded from networks available to others. Comments or assumptions based on your origin. Governed by specific anti-discrimination law separate from sexual harassment statutes.

**Sexual harassment** targets you based on gender: unwanted physical contact, quid pro quo propositions, hostile environment created through sexual behavior, supervisory coercion using attraction or intimacy.

**Intersectionality:** These do not always arrive separately. A woman of color in a predominantly white or male industry may face both racial discrimination and sexual harassment simultaneously. Research (Crenshaw, 1989; Hill Collins, 1990) shows that intersecting discrimination creates dynamics that neither framework alone fully describes. Harassers may also exploit racial stereotypes to amplify sexual coercion, or use cultural origin to isolate and discredit.

**How they are weaponized together:** Performance pressure deployed as cover for retaliation. Racial bias framed as "cultural fit" to build a dismissal case after a harassment complaint. A complaint met with claims you are "playing the discrimination card."

**How to stay clear:** Document each category separately and specifically. Ask whether comparable colleagues are treated the same way. Name each category explicitly when speaking to HR, legal counsel, or regulatory bodies. You do not have to choose one — your experience can be multi-dimensional, and your documentation can reflect that.

## What the Bystander Silence Means

When senior people witness harassment and do nothing, social psychology gives this a name: **bystander effect** (Darley & Latané, 1968). In groups, responsibility diffuses — everyone assumes someone else will act.

This is not neutral. Research on workplace culture shows that a single incident of visible harassment that goes unaddressed by authority figures increases the likelihood of future incidents in the same environment. The silence is a message: to the harasser, *this is acceptable here.* To everyone watching, *don't expect intervention.*

If you are in any senior position and you are reading this: you saw something and said nothing. That is a decision with consequences you passed on to someone with less power than you.

## What You Can Do

**In the moment:**
Name it out loud if you can. *"Don't touch me."* *"That is not appropriate."* Short, direct, no softening. If you cannot speak, move. Leave the space. Find anyone.

**Immediately after:**
Document. Date, time, location, names of anyone present, exact behavior. Write it to yourself by email.

**To report:**
At events: find staff, security, or organizers. Use the words *"I want this on record."* In workplaces: HR, legal, or external labor authority. If internal paths fail, external regulatory bodies and legal aid organizations exist.

---

## If You Are Threatened With Losing Your Job

Being told your employment depends on your silence or resignation is illegal retaliation in most jurisdictions. **Constructive dismissal** — engineering conditions hostile enough to force your resignation — is treated as wrongful termination.

Do not resign. Stay employed while you document. Collect everything showing treatment that changed after your refusal or complaint. Consult a labor attorney before signing anything.

---

## If You Are Being Threatened

A threat shows you exactly how much the other person knows they are in the wrong.

Do not delete anything. Screenshot, forward to a personal account, back it up. Do not respond directly — no negotiation, no apologies. Send one written statement: *"I am asking you not to contact me further."*

Report based on type: physical threats to police immediately. Blackmail is a criminal complaint — not just employment law. Workplace threats to HR and external labor authority with your documentation.

Tell someone you trust now. A time-stamped message describing what happened is meaningful documentation. The person making the threat has the legal exposure. You have the record.

## This Is Not Only Your Experience

The industries where power concentrates — finance, music, tech, crypto, any room where large money moves — have documented records of this pattern. These are not isolated incidents by unusual individuals. They are behaviors that survive because the environments around them have consistently chosen not to stop them.

You have every right to work. To take up space. To build a career without paying for it with your body, your silence, or your sense of safety.

Anyone using sex, power, race, or professional pressure to control you is not offering you an opportunity. They are telling you who they are.
